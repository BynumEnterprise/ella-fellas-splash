You've got the tickets and you've sorted the outfit. The last thing standing between you and a good night is what you actually carry in. So here's exactly what to bring to an Ella Langley concert in 2026 — and, just as important, what to leave in the car so you're not the person turned away at the bag check while your group goes in without you. We've done a handful of these shows on the Dandelion run this year; this is the list we'd hand a friend.

## The short version

If you read nothing else: bring a clear bag or no bag, a phone, a card, an ID, and earplugs. Leave the backpack, the big purse, and anything sharp at home. Most of the venues on this tour run a clear-bag policy, and the line moves a lot faster when you're not the holdup.

## Start with the bag rules — they decide everything else

Almost every arena and amphitheater on the 2026 Dandelion Tour uses some version of a clear-bag policy. The standard you'll see most often is a clear plastic, vinyl, or PVC tote up to about 12 inches by 6 inches by 12 inches, plus a small clutch roughly the size of your hand. Anything bigger or opaque gets sent back to your vehicle.

The single best move is to check your specific venue's policy the day before — search the building's name plus "bag policy," since the arena sets the rule, not the tour. If you'd rather skip the whole question, don't bring a bag at all. Pockets cover most of what you need, and you'll walk straight past the longest line at the door. If you do want a clear bag, buy a cheap stadium-approved one ahead of time rather than gambling on the one in your closet.

## What to actually bring

**Your phone, charged — and a small battery if you've got one.** Your ticket lives here, you'll want it for a few clips, and battery drains fast in a packed room hunting for signal. A compact power bank is the one "extra" we never regret. Set your ticket to download or screenshot it before you leave home, because the cell network outside a sold-out arena is a coin flip.

**A card and your ID.** Nearly every venue on this tour is cashless, so a single card or your phone's wallet covers parking, drinks, and the merch booth. Bring the ID even if you're not drinking — it speeds up anything age-related and you may need it at will-call.

**Earplugs.** This is the grown-up tip that sounds boring until you've stood near the speakers for "You Look Like You Love Me." A pair of reusable concert earplugs takes the harsh edge off without muffling the show, and they cost less than a beer. Your ears at breakfast will thank you.

**A light layer.** Arenas run cold during the openers and warm up fast once the floor fills in; amphitheaters can turn chilly after the sun drops. A thin jacket you can tie around your waist solves both. We get into the temperature swing in more detail in our [what to wear to an Ella Langley concert guide](/guides/what-to-wear-to-an-ella-langley-concert).

**Cash for one thing only: tips and parking backups.** It's mostly a cashless world now, but a few small bills cover a tip jar or a lot attendant who waves off cards. Keep it to a couple of singles.

## What to leave in the car (or at home)

Backpacks and full-size purses are the number-one reason people get pulled out of line — leave them behind. Skip outside food and drink, including sealed water bottles at most buildings; you can usually bring an empty bottle and fill it inside, but check first. Leave anything sharp, including the multi-tool on your keychain, and don't bother with a professional camera — phones are fine, detachable-lens cameras usually aren't. Selfie sticks, iPads, and umbrellas with a point all tend to get stopped too.
