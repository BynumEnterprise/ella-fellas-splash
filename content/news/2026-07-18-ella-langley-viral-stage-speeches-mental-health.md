# What Ella Langley Keeps Telling Us From the Stage

If you've caught an Ella Langley show this summer, you've probably noticed the set has a second gear that has nothing to do with the guitars. Somewhere in the middle of the night she'll pull the band down, grab the mic with both hands, and talk to the crowd like she's talking to one person. Those few minutes have been getting clipped and shared everywhere lately, and it's worth pulling them together — because the mental health honesty is quickly becoming as much a part of an Ella Langley concert as "Choosin' Texas."

## The social media speech that took off

The clip most people saw first came out of the Cowboys Music Festival in Calgary this month. Per [OutKick](https://www.foxnews.com/outkick-culture/ella-langley-tells-fans-stop-measuring-themselves-against-social-media-viral-concert-video), Ella stopped the show to go after the comparison trap that social media sets for everybody — the feeling that you're behind, or "not good enough, or not pretty enough, or not cool enough," that you can't sing or dance the right way. Her answer was blunt: "Who gives a sh*t? This is who I am, and that's what you get."

It landed because it didn't sound rehearsed. [American Songwriter](https://americansongwriter.com/this-is-who-i-am-ella-langleys-powerful-onstage-speech-for-anyone-who-feels-like-theyre-not-good-enough/) framed it as a message for anyone who's ever felt like they weren't enough, which is more or less the whole room at any given show. Coming from an artist whose album just did numbers almost nobody in country music has ever done, "this is who I am" hits differently than it would from a motivational poster.

## "It's okay to be two things"

What makes the speeches ring true is that Ella isn't performing invincibility. She's told crowds that even in the middle of all this — the No. 1s, the sold-out rooms, the awards — she's been struggling. In one stretch she called it "probably one of the toughest mental health weeks I've had in a long time," in the same breath as admitting "so many good things are happening, so many things I've prayed for and dreamed about."

That two-things-at-once idea is the thread. As she put it to [Audacy](https://www.audacy.com/national/music/ella-langley-on-struggling-with-mental-health-during-success), "it's okay to be two things" — grateful and exhausted, successful and scared, living your dream and white-knuckling your way through it. For a lot of us watching from the pit, that permission is the part that sticks.

## Where "Loving Life Again" fits in

None of this is new to Ella's story, and it's not a stage bit. It's the same well the music comes from. She's explained — most memorably during a Chattanooga show recapped by [American Songwriter](https://americansongwriter.com/ella-langley-reflects-on-mental-health-breakthrough-that-led-to-new-song-loving-life-again/) — that depression pushed her to cancel two weeks of shows and go home to her parents to get her feet back under her. Her friend and collaborator Ernest checked in when she came back, asked how she was doing, and she told him, "Well, looks like I'm back to loving life again." That line became the song.

That's why it means something that ["Loving Life Again" is the one she just sent to country radio](/news/2026-07-14-ella-langley-loving-life-again-radio-single). With a stack of swaggering hits to choose from, she picked the most vulnerable song on the record — the same message she keeps giving from the stage, just set to music. It's a straight line from the mic breaks to the single, and both trace back to the run that also produced ["Be Her," now the longest Country Airplay No. 1 by a solo woman](/news/2026-06-27-be-her-longest-country-airplay-no-1).

## Why the fans keep clipping it

Country crowds have heard plenty of "y'all are the best crowd ever" filler between songs. What Ella's doing is different, and the internet can tell. She's using the biggest platform of her life to say the least marketable thing possible: that the person you're comparing yourself to online is probably having their own rough week. If you're new to all this and want the backstory on how she got here, our guide to [who Ella Langley is](/guides/who-is-ella-langley) is a good place to start — and if you want to feel the speeches in person, the [Dandelion Tour dates](/tour) run through the rest of the year.

Whatever city she's in next, keep an eye on the middle of the set. That's usually where the best part of the night happens.
